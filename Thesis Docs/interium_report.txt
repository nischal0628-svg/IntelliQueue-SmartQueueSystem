1. Introduction
1.1 Current Scenario
Customer demand in the service sector in Nepal; the banking institutions, hospitals,
government offices, and the privately operated service centers have enjoyed a gradual
growth in customer demand as a result of population growth, urbanization, and the
demand to have efficient service delivery. Although this has increased, the majority of the
organizations still use the manual management techniques of queue management
including the use of paper tokens, physical queues and verbal announcements
(MarketsandMarkets, 2025).
These legacy systems are also becoming inefficient in processing large numbers of
service seekers especially when there is a peak in applications. Customers are forced to
spend long durations physically waiting without an idea of how much time they should
wait. This leads to the congestion of the waiting areas, wastage of customer time, and
bad service experience altogether. This inefficiency may have severe implications in
areas like healthcare and banking that may result in lost appointments, stress and work
bottlenecks.
There is also the absence of digital coordination that limits service providers to analyze
the queue patterns, to achieve effective staff allocation, and proactive demands control.
With the country moving towards digital transformation as part of the national efforts in
Digital Nepal Framework, the lack of intelligent queue management software is an
essential service modernization gap.
1.2 Problem Statement
The main issue that is tackled by this project is the fact that the service sector in Nepal
still relies on manual and semi-digital queue management systems. The systems lack
support of the current service demands like remote connectivity, real-time updates, and
smart prioritization.
• Manual dependency: Physical tokens and verbal calling systems are likely to fall
prey to human error, loss of tokens, duplication, and poor management.

Service Consumers (End Users)

3. Development Methodology
3.1 Considered Methodologies
To make a critical evaluation of the various development methodologies, some of the
most popular development methodologies were considered to be the most suitable to
develop the IntelliQueue system. The creation of the selected system was based on the
nature of the project, complexity of the system, changing requirements, real-time data
processing, and that the system is a single academic project being developed. The
methodologies that will be used are the Waterfall model, the Agile Scrum methodology
and the Extreme Programming (XP) (Sommerville, 2016).
3.1.1. Waterfall Methodology
The waterfall methodology can be described as a conventional, linear method of software
development that entails a progressive sequence of stages, which usually involves
requirement analysis, system design, implementation, testing, deployment, and
maintenance. The stages should be done one stage after another and very little feedback
is provided after a stage is done (Sommerville, 2016).
It was the first methodology to be considered as it has a clear structure and it places a
high emphasis on documentation, which satisfies the requirements of an academic
project. Waterfall model is also quite useful when one has these projects in which the
requirements are well defined, constant and are not likely to vary with time. It also eases
the planning of a project and tracking any progress, thus, becoming easier to show that
milestones have been met (Sommerville, 2016).
Nonetheless, the IntelliQueue is a user-focused and real-time system, which is based on
the constant improvement of the features as the queue logic, the notification time, and
the user experience. These elements must be tested regularly and improved with
repetitions depending on the observed behavior. The strict phase-based approach of the
Waterfall model is such that changes in requirements when development is already past
the design phase would be hard to implement. Also, early implementation feedback can
not be readily integrated without going through earlier steps which makes development
less risky but less flexible.

These shortcomings rendered the Waterfall approach to the IntelliQueue project as
inappropriate as it cannot facilitate progressive improvement and expediency
experimentation, which is essential to real-time queue control systems.
3.1.2 Agile Scrum Methodology
Agile Scrum is an iterative and incremental development model underpinning flexibility,
regular delivery of production software, and constant feedback with the stakeholders. The
development process is planned in brief cycles called sprints, and these sprints usually
take two to four weeks (Schwaber, 2020). Scrum has assigned certain roles like Product
Owner, Scrum Master and Development Team, as well as Scrum ceremonies such as
sprint planning, daily stand-ups, sprint reviews, and retrospectives.
Scrum was taken into consideration due to its flexibility to changes in requirements and
delivery of functional components that focus on small increments (Schwaber, 2020).
These features are quite in line with projects that entail changing user requirements and
real-time system functionality, including IntelliQueue. The sprint-based framework
enables one to review the progress periodically and constantly improve the functionality
of such aspects as the allocation of tokens, the control of the administrator, and the
process of notifications.
The Scrum however works best in team based developmental setting where roles and
responsibilities can be easily shared. In a single developer scholarly venture, the official
structure of Scrum brings about huge management overhead. Such activities like sprint
ceremonies and role separation are not very helpful in the single-developer environment
and can decrease the development efficiency. Furthermore, Scrum is not as focused on
technical practices like constant testing and refactoring that are very important in ensuring
reliability of real-time systems (Schwaber, 2020).
Subsequently, although Agile Scrum is flexible and adaptable, it was not chosen as the
main approach to be applied in this project because it has a procedural overhead and
because it cannot be effectively used in the development situation of a solo project.

3.2 Selected Methodology
3.2.1 Extreme Programming (XP)
Extreme Programming (XP) is an Agile software development approach that aims at
enhancing software quality and responsiveness to evolving requirements by releasing
software frequently, continuously testing it and integrating design and implementation.
XP fosters technical excellence by focusing on practices like incremental development,
continuous integration, refactoring and test driven development (Beck, 2004).
XP was deemed to be very appropriate in the IntelliQueue system since it focused on
brief development cycles, and quick feedback (Beck, 2004). The project consists of
several elements such as mobile application, admin dashboard, real-time database
operations, and AI-based wait-time estimation that have to be constantly validated and
refined. XP facilitates quick identification of problems in the design and performance
bottlenecks by constantly testing and refining.
Moreover, XP is most appropriate in the project that involves a single or small team
because it reduces the management overhead and enables the developer to concentrate
more on implementation and quality assurance (Beck, 2004). The approach can be used
to support requirement changes, which is also necessary in the case of IntelliQueue since
such aspects like priority processing, queue management, and notification policies can
be modified according to the results of testing and user feedback.
Continuous refactoring is a practice that makes the system architecture clean and
maintainable as the system is expanded. Incremental releases also enable the ability to
develop and test some system functionality such as authentication and simple queue
management with early stage of project lifecycle.
